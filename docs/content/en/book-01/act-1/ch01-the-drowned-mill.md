# Chapter One — The Drowned Mill

The crows had left Vrana, and that told Casimir more than the headman could.

He came down the cart-track at dusk with the smell already in his throat — not woodsmoke, not the green stink of standing water, but the other thing under it. Sweet and wrong, like meat left in a closed room. He had smelled it in marshes and byres and once in a chapel, and it had never once meant sickness. Sickness smelled of itself. This smelled of something pretending.

The hamlet was eight houses and a mill, hunched along a pond the colour of an old bruise. No dogs came out. No children. A woman stood in a doorway with a hatchet held low and crossways, and she did not lower it when he stopped.

"We've no plague here," she said. "Whatever they told you on the road."

"Nobody told me anything." He stayed in the saddle, which they liked better. A man on a horse could leave. "Your beasts go first?"

Her mouth tightened. That was answer enough.

"Cattle, then the pigs," she said at last. "Then Yedrek. The miller. Then his." She didn't say what *his* meant. She didn't have to. "We keep to our doors after dark and we pray. The father told us to pray."

"The father who came to bless the water."

The hatchet came up an inch. "Don't speak of him. He did us a kindness. He blessed the pond against the bad year and then the bad year came worse, and that is Svetar testing us, and you—" She stopped. Looked at the sword across his back — and kept looking, the way they all did, because it was not a thing you could help looking at. Too long, too broad, too heavy: a great ugly slab of dark metal, more cleaver than sword, old as a barrow and about as lovely, the kind of blade no sane smith would forge and no common man could lift off the ground. They always stared. They never knew what they were staring at. "You're the heretic," she said. "The one they hang stories on."

"I'm a man who'll end it," he said, "or I'm a man who'll ride on. Your choice, not mine."

---

They gave him the byre to sleep in because no one would take him under a roof, and that suited him. He didn't sleep. He sat in the dark with the door cracked and listened to the pond.

Healthy water has no voice past the frogs and the wind. This water *worked*. A slow churn, a wet sucking, like a great slack mouth opening and closing in its sleep. Twice in the night something broke the surface and went under again without a splash, just a fold of dark closing over dark.

At first light he walked the bank.

The reeds along the edge had gone black and greasy and stood too straight, as if something held them up from below. The mud was printed with tracks he knew and tracks he didn't — a cloven step gone wrong, the toes splayed and dragging, the gait of a thing that had once walked even and forgotten how. He crouched and pressed two fingers to the water and held them to the light. A film clung to them, pale and oily, and where it touched the cut on his knuckle the cut went cold, then numb, then began very quietly to ache the way old wounds ache before rain.

He wiped his hand on the grass and the grass curled where he wiped it.

Not plague. He'd been near enough to be sure now. Plague killed and was done. This didn't kill. It *kept*.

He worked it like any trade. He paced the bank and found the cold spot — every blighted place had one, a seam where the wrongness ran deepest — out past the mill-race where the pond went black and still and the reeds gave up entirely. He cut a green sapling, weighted the end with a stone in a sling of his own bootlace, and sounded the bottom there, slow, the way you'd sound for a drowned man.

Eight feet down the stone stopped on something that wasn't mud. Hard. It rang, faint and flat, up the length of the pole into his teeth — a sound iron makes, or stone, or a bell with no clapper. He held it there a moment, feeling the cold crawl up the green wood toward his hand, and then he drew it out before it could reach him, and the film on the pole had eaten the bark away to white.

Something was down there. Something the water had been poured over and made holy, and that holiness was rotting outward through the mud and the reeds and the cattle and the man named Yedrek, ring by ring, like rot from a buried thing.

He looked at the white pole a long while. He did not have a word for what he was looking at. He only knew it had not grown there. Somebody had *put* it there.

That was new. That was a thought he'd never had standing on a blighted bank before, and he didn't like the shape of it, the way it implied a hand.

He left it where it lay. The seed wasn't the work. The work was the miller.

---

They knew where Yedrek was. They'd known the whole time and let him find it himself, which was their way of keeping their hands clean of it.

The mill had a low stone cellar where the grain was kept dry, and they had put the miller down in it and walled him in alive. Field-stones stacked loose across the doorway, shoulder-high — not to keep a dead man in, but a living one. They could not bring themselves to kill him; he had been Yedrek, who ground their flour and stood godfather to half their children. And they could not bear to let him out, because Yedrek was gone and the thing wearing him was not. So they had bricked him up breathing and gone back to their prayers, and he had been down there in the black ever since — cursed, and alive. Casimir heard it as he pulled the stones down one at a time and set them aside, neat: a slow wet shifting in the dark below, and a sound that might once have been a man clearing his throat to speak. The smell came up out of the hole to meet him, that sweet pretending smell, thick enough now to taste.

He took the sword from his back before he went down. It came off the harness heavy as a felled branch, and it took both his hands to bring the length of it round at the cramped stair-head; even braced, the weight dragged at his shoulders. He drew it slow. It cleared the sheath with a sound like a long breath let go, and the cold of it ran straight up his arms into the meat of his shoulders, into his chest, the way it always did — a draw on something below the skin, a toll called before a single blow was struck. He set his teeth and let it take what it took. The cost was his to pay, and nothing smaller would bite the cursed deep enough to keep them down.

The thing in the cellar had been a big man once. It was bigger now, and wrong in its bigness, the bones gone long under skin the colour of the pond. It crouched in the grain-dust with its back to him and it was holding something — cradling it, a fold of sacking, rocking it slow against where a chest had been. It heard the stone and turned its head too far around and looked at him with eyes that had filmed over pale.

It opened its mouth. What came out was not a roar.

"*Lda,*" it said. Wet, half-shaped, a man's tongue learning ruin. It rocked the sacking. "*Mil—dla. Milada.*"

A name. The thing was murmuring a name into an empty fold of cloth, the way a man rocks a child that won't settle.

Casimir stood on the bottom step with the great blade's cold gnawing in his chest and did not move for the space of three breaths. He had a rule about that. You did not let it stop you. You let it cost you and you went on, because stopping was a kindness to yourself and a cruelty to them — to leave them like this, knowing the name and not the child, was the only true hell he believed in.

"Milada," he said, quiet, because the thing should hear it spoken once by a voice that wasn't ruined. "All right."

It came at him then, fast, the way they always turned fast at the end. There was no room to swing the great blade in that cellar and no breath in him to do it; he took the charge on the flat of it, staggered back a step against the stair, and then drove the point up under the long wrong ribs with both hands and all his back behind it — through, and on, and into the stone of the wall — and pinned it there, kicking and tearing, while the cold ran up the steel and out of the thing and into him until his vision went grey at the edges and the strength ran out of his legs like water.

It stopped. Slid down off the blade, slow, the dead weight of it near dragging the sword from his hands. The sacking fell open on the cellar floor, and there was nothing in it but old grain and dust.

Then the cost came due, the way it always did, all at once. He got the point of the blade down to the stone and leaned on the long pommel and shook. A cough tore up out of him, then another, racking, folding him double over the crossguard; his arms had gone to wet rope and his heart slammed as if he'd run a mile in harness. He stayed like that a long while, down on one knee in the dark, wrung empty, breathing the dust, until his legs would answer him again. They came back slow. They always came back a little slower than the time before — but that was a reckoning for later years, and he did not let himself think on it. He climbed the stairs hand over hand on the great sword like an old man on a stick, and he did not look at his hands, and he never looked.

---

They wouldn't pay him and he hadn't asked. The woman with the hatchet watched him saddle the horse and her face had nothing in it he could use — not thanks, not hate, just the flat closed look of people who would tell this story for years and make him the devil in it, because a devil they could understand was easier than a holy man who'd damned them.

"The water's clean now," she said. Not a question. A thing she needed to be true.

"The water's the same," he said. "There's a thing in the bottom of it, out past the race, that ought to come up and be broken. Burn what's left. Don't bless it. Don't let anyone bless it."

She didn't understand him and he hadn't expected her to. He could barely understand himself. He knew only that the seed had been *placed*, by a hand wearing a holy face, and that the placing was the wound — not the pond, not the miller, not the bad year. The kindness had been the killing thing. Somewhere a man had come with clean hands and good words and water in a bowl, and left this behind him, and ridden on.

He'd ridden on himself by midmorning, north, with the smell of Vrana thinning behind him and a name he hadn't been given turning over and over in his mouth like a stone.

*Milada.* He didn't know whose it was. He hoped, in the worn-out way he hoped things, that she'd been somewhere far off when the father came to bless the water.

He doubted it. Hands like that always saved the smallest for last.
